* Editor
Dear Eric Magar

Thank you for submitting your manuscript to Mexican Studies / Estudios Mexicanos. After receiving the reviewers’ evaluations and the assessment of the editorial team, we would like to invite you to revise the paper and resubmit for further review.

Please see this letter and the attached reviewer comments for further details about necessary revisions. Additionally, please note that your revised manuscript should be accompanied by a summary of your responses to the reviewers’ comments.

As you will see from the attached reviews, our external evaluators have submitted not merely positive but enthusiastic reviews, which is a testament to the importance of the data set that the article discusses and the way that it is presented. As you will also see, however, both reviewers express some uncertainty about whether quantitative research is a conventional or a good “fit” for MS/EM. I would add that the editorial team responded in a very similar way to the piece, which it finds to be remarkable on its own terms while being an unusual submission for this particular journal.

As editor I would like to address this issue and, in fact, make a proposal to you as author and compiler of the data set:

One of the things I have wanted to do as editor of an interdisciplinary journal like MS/EM is to create an occasional Research Methods or Research Notes section that would feature methodological type essays and not the more interpretive pieces that we typically publish. The idea would be to create, cumulatively, a kind of multidisciplinary toolkit that might be used, say, by researchers tackling new topics and who are interested in seeing how other more experienced colleagues have generated their research; by colleagues seeking cross-disciplinary insights to complement their own work; by graduate classes, etc.

Given the significance of your data set, I am wondering whether this might be the piece to begin such a section in MS/EM? If this kind of placement in the journal appeals to you, I would ask that in revising the piece you also add some additional narrative sections discussing (1): the reasons for and the problems encountered and resolved in creating the data set; (2) potential uses and significance of the data set; and (3) the methodological pros and cons of this data set in relation to others.

I do hope that this idea might appeal to you. Were we to launch a new section with your piece, we would also promote the piece with a blog and a period of free access, so that it would be more visible and more easily accessed in Mexico.

I normally offer authors eight weeks in which to resubmit your manuscript. However, in this case, I would be interested to hear from you in relation to the above before setting a deadline for revisions. Please feel free to reply either through the Discussion post in Scholastica or using the email address that trails this note.

Cordially,

* Reviewer 1
1. I see this research note as potentially publishable. The author has created a uniquely comprehensive dataset on municipal-level party politics in Mexico over several decades. This dataset will be a valuable resource for researchers studying the dynamics of party politics during an extraordinarily consequential period ion Mexico. The manuscript is well written, and results are presented through approachable visualizations.
   - *Actions taken*: None, the referee ask nothing from me here. 
2. The only suggestion for revision that I have is to ask that the author situate this dataset along similar databases from other countries. For example, comparable datasets may be found in the US (see, e.g., https://electionlab.mit.edu/data) and other democracies. Placing the author’s dataset in a broader cross-national context would help highlight for comparative political scientists the value of this project.
* Reviewer 2
1. This paper introduces an exceptional dataset of Mexican municipal election returns spanning almost five decades. The author has undertaken a remarkable feat of data collection, standardization, and public distribution, consolidating vote returns from dozens of disparate state-level sources into a single, accessible, and well-documented repository. The amount of work that went into assembling this dataset is hard to overstate (anyone who has tried to compile subnational electoral results can confirm this). My impression is that the dataset is already widely used by Mexican politics scholars, and this paper will serve as the authoritative reference for a resource that has already contributed to the field. I particularly appreciated the coalition files, incumbents dataset, and electoral calendar. These will substantially broaden the topics that researchers can study with the data (e.g., coattail effects, incumbency and reelection).
2. The paper is well-structured and the figures are excellent. In my view, the descriptive analysis does what a data note should do: it demonstrates the dataset’s content and potential using the data itself, and does so in ways that resonates with Mexican politics scholars.
3. These things said, I should note that I have no prior experience reviewing data notes of this kind. From the examples I am familiar with, such notes tend to move very quickly to the dataset itself, with little or no introductory material. Thus, I am genuinely uncertain on whether the opening sections (on municipal government structure, the parties, electoral fraud, etc.) are necessary or expected in this format. At the same time, it is also possible that the journal’s readership warrants more contextual framing than a general political science outlet would. To be clear, I only flag this as a question of fit with the journal’s conventions, and (of course) defer to the editor on the appropriate balance here.
4. I do have two more concrete suggestions: The data description in Section 4 could be made somewhat more self-contained. A brief but explicit summary of the key variables, their coverage, and missing data conventions within the paper itself (in addition to the external codebook) would strengthen the manuscript and make it more useful as a standalone reference.
5. The manuscript requires careful proofreading. There are several typos that should be corrected before publication.
